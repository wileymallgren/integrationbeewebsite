King's Rule

King's Rule is a method in which you replace every variable of integration in a definite integral with the upper bound, plus the lower bound, minus the variable of integration. \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \int_a^b f(a + b - x)\,dx \]

Why is it useful?

King's Rule is useful when making the change above will simplify the integral. This is especially useful for trigonometric integrals in which co-function identities can be used to simplify the integral.

Example

Evaluate the integral:

\[ \int_0^{\pi} \frac{x \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx \]
At first glance, this is a messy integral with no easy antiderivative. But King’s Rule gives us a way in:

Using King’s Rule: \[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \int_a^b f(a + b - x)\,dx \] For this problem, \( a = 0 \), \( b = \pi \), so \( a + b - x = \pi - x \). Apply it:

\[ \int_0^{\pi} \frac{x \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{(\pi - x) \sin(\pi - x)}{1 + \cos^2(\pi - x)} \, dx \]
Now use trigonometric identities: \[ \sin(\pi - x) = \sin(x), \quad \cos^2(\pi - x) = \cos^2(x) \] So the reflected integral becomes:

\[ \int_0^{\pi} \frac{(\pi - x) \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx \]
Now call the original integral \( I \):

\[ I = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{x \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx \] And from King’s Rule, we also have:

\[ I = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{(\pi - x) \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx \]
Add both expressions for \( I \):

\[ 2I = \int_0^{\pi} \left( \frac{x \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} + \frac{(\pi - x) \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \right) dx \]
\[ = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{\pi \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx \]
\[ 2I = \pi \int_0^{\pi} \frac{\sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx \]
Now solve the simpler integral using the substitution \( u = \cos(x) \), \( du = -\sin(x)dx \):

Change of limits: when \( x = 0 \), \( u = 1 \); when \( x = \pi \), \( u = -1 \]

\[ \int_0^{\pi} \frac{\sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx = -\int_1^{-1} \frac{1}{1 + u^2} \, du = \int_{-1}^1 \frac{1}{1 + u^2} \, du \]
\[ = \tan^{-1}(1) - \tan^{-1}(-1) = \frac{\pi}{4} - \left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{\pi}{2} \]
Substitute back:

\[ 2I = \pi \cdot \frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi^2}{2} \Rightarrow I = \frac{\pi^2}{4} \]
Final Answer:
\[ \int_0^{\pi} \frac{x \sin(x)}{1 + \cos^2(x)} \, dx = \frac{\pi^2}{4} \]

Tabular Method (IBP)

The Tabular Method is a method of integration by parts that is useful for integrals of the form \( \int u \, dv \) where \( u \) and \( dv \) are both easy to differentiate and integrate, respectively. It is a faster way to do integration by parts than the standard method. this is especially true for repeated integration by parts.

The Method

1. Choose \( u \) and \( dv \):
- Select \( u \), the function to differentiate (typically, choose a polynomial or simpler function).
- Select \( dv \), the function to integrate (typically an exponential, trigonometric, or algebraic function).

2. Differentiate \( u \) and integrate \( dv \):
- Keep differentiating \( u \) and integrating \( dv \) until the derivative of \( u \) becomes zero, until you can integrate going across, or until you get in a loop
- Alternate the signs of the terms, starting with a positive sign.

3. Construct the Tabular Table:
Use the following format for the table:

$$ \begin{array}{c|c} \text{Differentiate } u & \text{Integrate } dv \\ \hline u_1 & v_1 \\ u_2 & v_2 \\ u_3 & v_3 \\ \vdots & \vdots \\ u_n & v_n \\ \end{array} $$

4. Apply the Integration by Parts Formula:
Use the formula:

$$ \int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du $$

5. Combine the Results:
Multiply the corresponding \( u \) and \( v \) terms diagonally from the table. Alternate the signs and combine the results to complete the integral.

Final Result:
The solution will be of the form:

$$ \int u \, dv = \text{(first term)} - \text{(remaining integral)} + C $$

This method is very helpful for simplifying integrals that involve the product of a polynomial and an exponential or trigonometric function.

Example

We want to compute the integral:

\[ \int x^2 e^x \, dx \]

Let:

  • \( u = x^2 \) (to be differentiated)
  • \( dv = e^x \, dx \) (to be integrated)

We construct a table:

Sign Derivative (u) Integral (dv)
+ \( x^2 \) \( e^x \)
- \( 2x \) \( e^x \)
+ \( 2 \) \( e^x \)
- \( 0 \) \( e^x \)

Now apply the tabular integration formula:

\[ \int x^2 e^x \, dx = x^2 e^x - 2x e^x + 2 e^x + C \]

Final Answer:

\[ \boxed{e^x(x^2 - 2x + 2) + C} \]

\(n-n=0\)

Though this trick has no formal name (that I am aware of), it is important to know. This basically boils down to knowing that you can add a number to something if you also subtract it. This is best seen by the example below.

Example

$$\int \frac{2x + 1}{2x + 2} \, dx$$


Rewrite the numerator to help simplify:
$$2x + 1 = (2x + 2) - 1$$
So:
$$\frac{2x + 2 - 1}{2x + 2} = \frac{2x + 2}{2x + 2} - \frac{1}{2x + 2}$$
$$= 1 - \frac{1}{2(x + 1)}$$


Integrate term by term:
$$\int \left(1 - \frac{1}{2(x + 1)}\right) dx = \int 1 \, dx - \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{x + 1} \, dx$$


Evaluate each integral:
$$\int 1 \, dx = x,\quad \int \frac{1}{x + 1} \, dx = \ln|x + 1|$$


Final Answer:
$$\boxed{x - \frac{1}{2} \ln|x + 1| + C}$$

Weirstrass Substitution

The Weierstrass substitution is a technique used to simplify the integration of trigonometric functions. It is particularly useful for integrals involving sine and cosine functions. The substitution is given by: \[ t = \tan\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) \] From this we get the following: \[ \sin(x) = \frac{2t}{1 + t^2} \] \[ \cos(x) = \frac{1 - t^2}{1 + t^2} \] \[ dx = \frac{2dt}{1 + t^2}\] Similar substitutions can be found for the other three trigonometric functions.

Interestingly, this substitution follows the same pattern for hyperbolic trigonometric function except addition and subtraction are flipped

Why is it useful?

The Weierstrass substitution is useful because it allows us to convert trigonometric integrals into rational functions, which are often easier to integrate. This is especially helpful when dealing with integrals that involve products or powers of sine and cosine.

Example

Let’s evaluate the integral: \[ \int \frac{dx}{1+\sec x} \] using the Weierstrass substitution.

First, note the substitution: \[ t = \tan\frac{x}{2}, \quad dx = \frac{2\,dt}{1+t^2}, \quad \sec x = \frac{1+t^2}{1-t^2}. \] Substitute these into the integral: \[ \int \frac{dx}{1+\sec x} = \int \frac{1}{1+\frac{1+t^2}{1-t^2}} \cdot \frac{2\,dt}{1+t^2}. \] Simplify the denominator term: \[ 1+\frac{1+t^2}{1-t^2} = \frac{(1-t^2)+(1+t^2)}{1-t^2} = \frac{2}{1-t^2}. \] Thus, the integral becomes: \[ \int \frac{dx}{1+\sec x} = \int \frac{1-t^2}{2} \cdot \frac{2\,dt}{1+t^2} = \int \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2}\,dt. \] Separate the fraction: \[ \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2} = 1 - \frac{2t^2}{1+t^2}, \] So that: \[ \int \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2}\,dt = \int 1\,dt - 2\int \frac{t^2}{1+t^2}\,dt. \] Notice that: \[ \frac{t^2}{1+t^2} = 1 - \frac{1}{1+t^2}, \] Which gives: \[ \int \frac{t^2}{1+t^2}\,dt = t - \arctan t. \] Therefore, combining results: \[ \int \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2}\,dt = t - 2\left(t - \arctan t\right) + C = -t + 2\arctan t + C. \] Substituting back \( t = \tan\frac{x}{2} \), we obtain: \[ -\tan\frac{x}{2} + 2\arctan\!\Bigl(\tan\frac{x}{2}\Bigr) + C. \] Assuming the principal value \( \arctan\!\left(\tan\frac{x}{2}\right) = \frac{x}{2} \), the final answer is: \[ \boxed{x - \tan\frac{x}{2} + C.} \]
